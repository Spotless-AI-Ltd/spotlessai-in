Role of Microbiomes in Healthcare Settings

The Vital Role of Microbiomes in Healthcare Settings: Understanding, Managing, and Preventing Healthcare-Associated Infections


In the complex world of microorganisms, the microbiome occupies a significant place. This vast community of trillions of microbes, including bacteria, viruses, fungi, and protozoa, harbors in and on our bodies. While the human gut microbiome often hogs the limelight, microbiomes are ubiquitous - found in a wide range of environments, including healthcare facilities.


In healthcare settings, these microbial communities can have both beneficial and harmful implications. This blog post aims to shed light on the various microbiomes frequently observed in these environments, their association with healthcare-associated infections (HAIs), and how continuous disinfection can reduce these risks.


Understanding the Microbiomes in Healthcare Settings

Healthcare facilities are teeming with a diverse array of microbiomes. These range from those found on surfaces like bedrails, countertops, and medical equipment, to those living on and inside healthcare workers, patients, and visitors. Notable microbiomes frequently observed include:

  • Staphylococcus

    A common bacterium found on the skin and nasal passages, it can cause serious infections if it enters the body.

  • Clostridioides difficile (C. difficile)

    A bacterium that causes severe diarrhea and colitis, often associated with prolonged antibiotic use.

  • Enterococci

    Often found in the human gut, some strains, like VRE (Vancomycin-resistant Enterococci), are resistant to several antibiotics.

  • Escherichia coli (E. coli)

    While many strains are harmless and live in the human gut, some can cause serious infections.

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa

    A common bacterium that can cause severe infections, particularly in people with weakened immune systems.


These microbes can cause HAIs, a significant cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide. Recent data suggest that approximately 1.7 million HAIs occur in U.S. hospitals each year, leading to around 99,000 deaths.

Microbiomes and HAIs: An Interconnected Web


These microbiomes become particularly problematic when they mutate into antibiotic-resistant strains, often due to the inappropriate use of antibiotics. They pose severe risks, especially to immunocompromised individuals. Unsurprisingly, the healthcare environment, with its high usage of antibiotics and prevalence of vulnerable individuals, often acts as a hub for these resistant strains.


Transmission of these harmful microbes can occur via multiple routes: direct patient-to-patient contact, healthcare workers, or contaminated surfaces and medical equipment. A 2020 study published in the journal 'Infection Control & Hospital Epidemiology' provided new insights into the role of hospital room surfaces in microbial transmission. It suggested that contamination of specific high-touch surfaces was a risk factor for transmission of pathogens to subsequent patients.


The Importance of Disinfection in Managing Microbiomes


Disinfection plays a crucial role in managing microbiomes in healthcare settings, aiming to minimize HAIs. Commonly used methods include:

  • Cleaning with disinfectants

    Regular cleaning of surfaces with EPA-registered disinfectants is effective in killing many harmful microbes.

  • Sterilization

    Critical items that come into contact with sterile body areas are often sterilized to eliminate all forms of microbial life.

  • Antimicrobial surfaces

    The use of surfaces made from copper alloys, known for their antimicrobial properties, can reduce surface contamination.


Preventing the Spread of Harmful Microbiomes


While disinfection is crucial, it's only part of the solution to preventing HAIs. A comprehensive infection control program should encompass the following:


  • Antibiotic stewardship

    Aims at promoting the appropriate use of antibiotics to combat antibiotic resistance.

  • Hand hygiene

    Regular and proper handwashing by healthcare workers, patients, and visitors can significantly reduce transmission.

  • Vaccination

    Certain HAIs can be prevented by vaccination, such as the Hepatitis B vaccine for healthcare workers.

  • Isolation precautions

    Patients with certain infections may need to be placed in isolation to prevent transmission.

In addition to these, emerging research points towards a future where we might manipulate the healthcare microbiome itself to combat HAIs. Recent studies suggest that introducing benign or beneficial microbes could potentially outcompete harmful ones.


Conclusion: A Proactive Approach to Reducing HAIs:
Healthcare-associated infections pose a significant public health challenge. Understanding and managing the healthcare microbiome is essential in controlling these infections. A multi-pronged approach involving disinfection, prevention measures, and potentially microbiome manipulation may pave the way towards a safer healthcare environment.
